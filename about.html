About Us

Meet the Creator: Artin Forouzan

The leading cause of death worldwide from organ rejection and failure is rooted in a simple yet profound issue: inequitable access to information and healthcare services. Solving this problem requires more than financial support or system upgrades. It calls for a new generation of well-rounded, innovative leaders committed to global change.

Seeking to become one of these leaders, I reached out to SUNY Upstate Medical Hospital to further my knowledge and experience. At first, the responses were “We are full” or “You are too young.” But I kept pushing and eventually earned one of the youngest internship positions at the hospital. I flew to New York to shadow the head of transplant surgery.

Right after arriving, I began to regret taking a red-eye flight. The moment I opened my hotel door, my phone buzzed with news that an organ had become available and the transplant team was heading into surgery. Excited, I called an Uber and rushed to the hospital. After completing the formalities, I put on my first set of scrubs. Words cannot describe how I felt. At sixteen, entering my junior year of high school, I never imagined I would scrub in to observe a transplant happening right before my eyes.

Even though I was thrilled, I was also scared. Walking into that OR, surrounded by complex machines and people far more experienced than me, felt like entering a war zone. But to my surprise, it was the opposite. Instead of the tense, quiet scene I expected from movies, spicy tango music played from a speaker on the wall. Everyone was relaxed and confident, going about their work with ease. That’s when I realized these duties that seem so daunting to others had become normal for them.

For the next three weeks, I shadowed the transplant head, met patients, and got to know the residents. When my time ended, I wanted to do more—even if from behind a computer screen. I connected with the research team and joined a retrospective study on antiviral therapies over the last twenty years and their effect on hepatitis C after transplantation. After months of work, Zoom meetings, and data analysis, we finished the paper. After final revisions, it was published in the Journal of Clinical and Experimental Hepatology.

You might be tired of hearing about my experience, but what I want to share is this: through effort and communication, I achieved more than I dreamed and explored parts of a field I hope to study further. That’s why I created this site. Vitals and Voices gives other students the chance I fought for—a place to express curiosity, explore passions, and contribute meaningfully to health and medicine. You don’t need a title or degree for your perspective to matter. Whether you have shadowed a surgeon, cared for a loved one, researched a health issue, or just have a story, your voice belongs here.

This platform is more than a blog. It is a community of students who believe change begins with conversation, reflection sparks innovation, and storytelling is as vital as science. Whether you are here to read, write, or be inspired, welcome. Let’s build something meaningful together.
